Heatmapping Congress

A congressional district map tool showing campaign contributions by PACS

Proof of Concept

This map shows the contributions made by Corrections Corporation of America between 2011-2012 by congressional district.

Try it yourself!

Next Steps

- Integrate social media.
- Allow highlighting based on committee membership.
- Enable maps to saved to public galleries and embedded in other sites.
- Allow multiple PACs to be visualized on the same output map.
- Visualize contributions from individuals, aggregated by employer.
- Visualize past contributions. (Congress was redistricted for the 113th Congress. Thus, we need to create another map to properly map contributions made in previous cycles.)
- Visualize PAC contributions made to losing candidates. (PAC donors are pretty good at picking the winners, but certainly not perfect.)
- Link to associated static FEC Committee page, Open Secrets PAC profile, Influence Explorer profile, Company Website, NYSE/NASDAQ website, etc.
- Build functionality to map contributions made to Senate members during the 2011-2012 cycle and previous cycles. (Because the Senate is state-by-state, it is less complicated than buiding functionality for a House of Representatives map. Because the Senate is never redistricted, the same underlying map will be applicable for all cycles.)

Limitations

- Mapping this money does not make the money or influence go away. - Mapping this money does not prove that this money influences the elected officials who receive it.
- Mapping this money (as well as other transparency efforts) may encourage contributors to redirect their money into the dark.
- There is a LOT of contributor money that is now in the dark, and inherently unmappable.
- Money has long influenced U.S. politics. But the Supreme Court’s 2010 decision in Citizens United v. Federal Election Commission opened the floodgates. The decision effectively granted corporations the right to give unlimited funds to oppose or support candidates through “independent expenditures”—funds that are not given directly to candidates or political parties. Super PACs must report donors, but can accept funds from anonymous sources when passed through nonprofit groups that profess to have a social mission. The anonymous “dark money” spent through this new loophole amounted to over $400 million in the 2011-2012 cycle. Source
